• Patients may require blood tests for several months after stopping treatment.

  • Stopping treatment in patients with advanced liver disease or cirrhosis is not recommended as it may lead to worsening of hepatitis, which can be life-threatening.

  • Tell your doctor immediately about new or unusual symptoms after stopping treatment, particularly those associated with hepatitis B infection.

  • Possible side effects include signs of inflammation or infection, autoimmune disorders, muscle weakness, palpitations, tremor, and hyperactivity.

  • Common side effects may affect up to 1 in 10 people and include depression, abnormal dreams, headache, dizziness, diarrhoea, feeling sick, tiredness, anaemia, vomiting, stomach pain, and joint pain.

  • Uncommon side effects may affect up to 1 in 100 people and include Stevens-Johnson syndrome (SJS), which is a serious life-threatening condition.

  • Rare side effects may affect up to 1 in 1000 people and include osteonecrosis, which is a bone disease that can cause death of bone tissue caused by loss of blood supply to the bone.

  • During HIV therapy, there may be an increase in weight and levels of blood lipids and glucose.

  • Report any side effects to your doctor or pharmacist. You can also report side effects directly via the national reporting system listed in Appendix V.

Please consult with your doctor if you have any further questions on the use of this medicine.
